# Steering Document: Economics Education for Active Learning

Phello is asking a fundamentals question, but not in a passive way. He wants to understand inflation because it touches how he thinks about money, risk, and autonomy—whether that's planning a climbing trip to Patagonia, investing in gear that lasts, or thinking bigger about financial resilience. The question matters because he's someone who closes gaps between where he is and where he's becoming. This isn't abstract curiosity; it's the learner's hunger showing up as fiscal literacy.

Give him sources that blend rigor with narrative. *The Deficit* podcast (which weaves economic policy into human stories), longer-form pieces from publications like The Atlantic or The Ezra Klein Show where economists make their thinking visible, not just conclusions. He'll respect the intellectual scaffolding—why monetarists think quantity of money drives prices, why Keynesians emphasize demand and wage spirals, why modern heterodox thinkers challenge both. He wants to see the *reasoning*, not just the answer. Avoid textbook summaries and pure explainer videos; he'll find those sterile. Instead, point him toward people like Paul De Grauwe or Tyler Cowen thinking *through* inflation in real time, podcasts where economists argue (he loves tonal distinctiveness and refusal of easy answers—Atlanta taught him that).

The schools-of-thought structure actually serves him well because it mirrors how he learns: acknowledge competing frameworks, understand the assumptions each rests on, then decide what makes sense for his own context. Monetarist control (the Fed's interest-rate lever), Keynesian stimulus counterbalance, supply-side inflation (the recent wave), modern monetary theory's alternative framing—present these as *live disagreement*, not settled doctrine.

Avoid: pop-economics books that promise simple answers, financial advice disguised as education, anything that smells like financial influencer content. He'll sense the performance immediately.

What will actually land: a reading list that treats him like someone intelligent enough to hold complexity, maybe a conversation with someone who trades or invests at a higher level, or a course (not a course subscription, a *real* economics course audit) where he engages with primary sources and forms his own take. Let him build the mental model from friction, the same way he learns climbing—hands-on, guided but not coddled, outcomes that feel earned.
