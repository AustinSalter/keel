He lives in the tension between dirt and polish, and that tension is the whole point. He's the guy who'll sleep crumpled in the back of a car at a trailhead, chalk still on his hands, then show up two nights later in a tux looking like he was born in one — and neither version is performance. Both are native. Climbing and surfing aren't hobbies bolted onto his life; they're the organizing grammar of it. Travel gets planned around crags and swells, meals get scheduled around tides and daylight, and the best trip is one where his body is wrecked by sundown. He seeks the visceral over the curated — cold water, overhead waves, exposed rock — not because he romanticizes suffering but because intensity is how he knows he's paying attention.

Yet there's nothing monastic about him. He calibrates atmosphere with real precision: loud and buzzing when the crew's together, candlelit and unhurried when it matters. A wagyu ribeye and a well-made cocktail aren't contradictions to the dirtbag ethos — they're evidence that he treats pleasure as seriously as he treats challenge. He wants to feel things fully, whether that's a coastal highway with his family or a quiet neighborhood café discovered on an electric bike with no itinerary.

He's drawn to people and brands that embody purpose without pretension — the Patagonia pilgrimages, the podcast stories of people building unconventional lives. There's a learner's hunger underneath everything, a quiet belief that the right book or tool or conversation could unlock the next version of himself. The morning-person aspiration isn't trivial; it's a tell — he's someone who maps the gap between who he is and who he's becoming and genuinely enjoys closing it.

Family isn't separate from the adventure; it's woven through it. Redwoods, coastlines, road trips — these are how he builds shared memory, how love gets expressed in motion rather than stillness. He recharges through friction with rock and water during the week, then turns outward on weekends, toggling between solitude and social energy with an ease that suggests he actually knows himself. He doesn't want the postcard version of anywhere. He wants the version that leaves sand in his shoes, chalk on the steering wheel, and a story worth retelling.
