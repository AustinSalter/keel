# Steering Document: Conviction & Company Building

The question itself tells you something about him: he's asking not *whether* conviction matters, but *when it becomes dangerous*—a distinction that tracks his whole operating system. He doesn't want ideology; he wants calibration. The tension he lives in (polish and dirt, solitude and crew) is exactly the tension that kills startups and makes them, and he's smart enough to know the difference.

His frame is this: conviction without *feedback loops* is just ego with a business plan. He respects founders who move with force—Patagonia's example sits with him precisely because they didn't ask permission to make gear that lasted, or to take environmental positions that threatened quarterly earnings. But he's also slept uncomfortable nights and learned that the body tells you when you're pushing into injury versus growth. He'll recognize that conviction is the fuel, but feedback—from users, from market resistance, from collaborators who'll push back—is the instrument panel.

What to emphasize: The people he admires most (the unconventional builders he listens to) succeed because they hold conviction *lightly* about tactics while staying rigid about principle. A founder can be absolutely certain about *why* the problem matters while remaining radically uncertain about *how* to solve it. That's the distinction. When conviction calcifies into "we know the answer," it stops being navigation and becomes blindness. The liability isn't the strength of belief—it's the refusal to let reality reshape the path. He'll hear this viscerally: a climber who commits fully to a route but abandons it immediately when the rock says no. That's not weakness. That's attention.

Avoid framing conviction as binary (fanatic vs. pragmatist). He won't buy it, and it won't help him. Instead, show him examples of founders who toggled—who believed hard, faced hard truths, and reconsidered *methods* while keeping *mission* intact. He'll also need permission to recognize that the person building the company needs exactly the same feedback loops he uses on the rock: is this fatigue telling me something real, or am I just tired? He's primed to understand that conviction without self-knowledge is arrogance. The morning-person aspiration signals he's working on that calibration in his own life. A startup is the same muscle.
