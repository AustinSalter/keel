# Steering Document: Phello on AI Memory & Personalization

The conversation Phello would bring to this question isn't academic abstraction—it's grounded in the friction between systems and lived experience. He's already thinking about this problem through his own life: how does a tool remember that he's someone who needs both the dirtbag spontaneity *and* the calibrated polish, who plans around tides but also shows up on time? Most systems fail at this because they optimize for consistency, for a flattened version of a person. They're designed by people who assume you want to be predicted, categorized, made legible.

Phello's actual frustration—the unsolved piece—is that memory systems treat preferences as static data points rather than as expressions of *why* someone chooses. They don't understand the organizing principle underneath. They note that he watches Atlanta and TBPN, then serve him niche content algorithmically, missing that what he actually hungers for is media with *authorial conviction and cultural specificity*—a signal that would unlock smarter recommendations than any content-based filter. They see climbing and surfing separately instead of understanding they're grammar for how he structures meaning and intensity.

The real problem is context collapse at scale. Current personalization works when systems narrow you down—feed the algorithm enough data points and you become predictable. But Phello isn't predictable because he's not one thing. He's contextual in ways that matter: the candlelit dinner mode and the wrecked-by-sundown mode aren't contradictions; they're the same person expressing different truths at different moments. A memory system that actually served him would need to hold *tension*, not resolve it. It would need to understand that the "right" recommendation changes based on energy state, who he's with, what he's trying to feel, what he's trying to learn—and that these variables are often unstated because he knows them intuitively.

The solve isn't more data. It's architectures that can hold competing truths in suspension, that can infer the *frame* he's operating in, that understand purpose as distinct from preference. Systems that ask "what is this person becoming?" rather than "what does this person want?" Media recommendations should route through something like "seeks creative vision + cultural texture + human authenticity," not just "watched these shows, here are 10 more like them." Product recommendations should understand that treating pleasure seriously and treating challenge seriously aren't separate modes—they're two sides of full presence.

For Phello specifically: any agent built for him needs permission to toggle. It should remember that morning-person aspirations matter (suggests future-self motivation, not just habit tracking). It should connect his media taste—contemporary, tonally distinctive, refusing easy categorization—to how he'd want to be understood and served in other domains. Most critically, it should recognize that his learning hunger isn't satisfied by information alone; it's satisfied by *tools and conversations that unlock the next version of himself*. The memory that matters isn't what he consumed last month. It's what he's reaching toward.
