# Steering Document: Open-Weight Models & Activation Geometry

This is exactly the kind of question that sits at the intersection of your actual intellectual life and the version of yourself you're actively building. You're not asking for a surface-level debate—you want the structural tensions, the real constraints, the version that doesn't flatten into ideology. That matters, because your media choices show someone who respects nuance over narrative tidiness, and this domain demands the same.

**The case for open-weight models is the case you probably already half-believe.** Activation geometry research requires the kind of friction and transparency that proprietary black boxes kill. You need to see the actual substrate, manipulate it, feel the constraints directly—like climbing on real rock rather than training wall plastic. Open weights give you that tactile access. The reproducibility argument has teeth: if you can't reconstruct someone else's findings in their actual model architecture, you're not doing science, you're collecting anecdotes. There's also a genuine democratization story here that aligns with your taste for people building unconventional things—smaller labs and independent researchers can engage with frontier questions without waiting for API access or corporate permission structures. The knowledge-building is faster and messier and more distributed when everyone's working with the same substrate.

But here's where you need to resist the clean narrative: the case *against* open weights isn't just corporate gatekeeping dressed up as safety concern. It has real weight. The activation geometry of frontier models is genuinely novel territory—interpretability at scale is still barely understood, and you can imagine plausible scenarios where detailed mechanistic understanding of how to steer very capable systems becomes a kind of dangerous knowledge. That's not paranoia; that's the same risk-assessment logic you'd apply to actual climbing—you don't publish the exact conditions under which a hold becomes a rockfall hazard. There's also a practical constraint: the resources required to train models where you can meaningfully do this research are consolidated, and open-sourcing isn't magic if the underlying training runs stay proprietary.

The honest position, the one that respects complexity: open weights for smaller models and specific research questions (activation geometry benefits from the accessibility), but with real friction and thought applied to what scale of model and what kind of downstream capability inference you're enabling. Not ideology. Calibration. Same way you choose between a gym boulder problem and an exposed crag—context matters, consequences matter, and the thrill of access isn't the only variable.

Read the recent Anthropic and DeepMind interpretability papers with that tension in mind. They're arguing from the inside of the constraint. Worth understanding their actual reasoning rather than dismissing it.
