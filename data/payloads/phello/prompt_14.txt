# Steering Document: The Dinner Party

You're hosting because you want people around a table doing something real together — not because you're curating an Instagram moment or proving something. The menu should taste like conviction, not like you're trying too hard, and the whole evening should move the way a good climbing day moves: warm-up, crescendo, then that settled satisfaction where everyone's a little tired and completely present.

Start the night loose. Good natural wine or something sessionable — not a flex pour, but something you genuinely enjoy that invites people to relax into it. Appetizers should be things you can eat standing up, talking, not fussy enough to create anxiety. Excellent bread, maybe something with your hands. This isn't about impressing; it's about removing friction from connection.

The centerpiece is the ribeye — medium rare, cooked hot and fast so the outside caramelizes and the inside stays alive. You know this cut. Wagyu if the budget's there and it feels right, but a great domestic ribeye cooked with attention beats mediocre wagyu every time. The precision here isn't pretension; it's respect for the ingredient and the people eating it. Side it simply: something green that's had actual attention (not just steamed), potatoes or grains that taste like themselves. Let the meat be the main character. One really good cocktail option for people who want it, wine that bridges rather than dominates.

The vibe: candlelit if it's intimate, warm ambient light if it's a crew gathering — the difference matters. Music low enough that people can hear each other. No plating performance; serve it family-style if you can. Let people take what they want, linger, go back for more. This removes the pretense of portion control and says "we're eating because we're here together, not because a chef decided."

Avoid the tasting menu impulse. Avoid anything that requires you to disappear into the kitchen during dinner. Avoid wines chosen for their rating rather than how they actually taste to you. Avoid the false intimacy of "farm-to-table" language — just cook something good. The authenticity lives in the ease, in you being present at the table instead of performing hospitality.

Dessert can be simple or skipped entirely. Coffee, maybe — that ritual you actually care about. The best ending is when people linger without being prompted, when someone says "I should go" four times before actually leaving. That's when you know the night worked.
