# Steering Document: Training vs. Play

The question itself contains a false binary that you'll want to dissolve rather than optimize. For him, structure and unstructure aren't opposing forces—they're partners in a conversation about intention and attention.

His natural mode is already deeply structured: climbing and surfing are brutally disciplined activities masked by spontaneity. A big-wave session is chaos only if you're untrained; to him, it's a sophisticated system of reading swells, positioning, timing, reading his body's feedback. Same with climbing—the "play" of bouldering on a random crag is only free if he's built a foundation of movement literacy first. So the real tension isn't training versus play; it's *purposeful structure versus purposeless wandering*, and he instinctively avoids the latter.

What he should hear: lean into periodization that respects seasonal rhythms and actual goals rather than generic programming. If he wants to surf bigger waves, he needs specific shoulder and core resilience work—not as boring prerequisite but as the scaffolding that lets him survive and thrive in the water he actually craves. If climbing at grade X is the goal, some sessions need to be methodical, repetitive, sometimes indoors under fluorescent lights. But the structure should always be *tethered to something he genuinely wants to do*, not abstract fitness.

The unstructured play is where he finds renewal and discovery—the pre-dawn beach paddle, the random boulder problem on a new crag that teaches him something about his body he didn't know. That's not wasted time; that's where intuition develops. Protect those sessions fiercely. But notice: even "unstructured" play is rarely aimless for him. He's *attending*—reading conditions, experimenting with technique, noticing what his body can do.

**The pragmatic move:** Build a training architecture with enough flexibility to chase conditions (good swell, prime climbing season, unexpected opportunities) without collapsing into zero structure. He's a morning-person aspirant, so early sessions before work or family time can be training-focused (strength, technique, deliberate practice), while evening or weekend sessions stay more play-oriented. That distinction matters; it gives him permission to be serious about both.

Avoid generic "balanced routine" advice or anything that treats his sports as secondary to conditioning. He'll smell that immediately and dismiss it. Instead, frame training as the thing that unlocks the next level of play—the sessions that let him survive overhead tubes or onsight harder rock. That's intrinsically motivating for someone built like him.
