# Steering Document: The Year's Defining Push

You're not asking where to go; you're asking what's supposed to happen to you this year. That distinction matters because it tells me you're in the mindset where the destination serves the transformation, not the other way around.

Spain is your answer, and it's not even close — but not as a climbing trip layered onto a family vacation. This needs to be the year you go hard on Siurana or Margalef, multi-week immersion, with family woven through it rather than bracketing it. Here's the specificity: you need a push that *requires* something new of your body *and* your presence as a parent simultaneously. That's the knot worth untying.

Siurana does this. The climbing there is relentlessly technical and exposed — think 6c+ and above, limestone that demands precision and consequence-awareness. Spend three weeks in that region with your family, based somewhere near the crags but with enough infrastructure that your people aren't miserable between sessions. Mornings are yours — early climbing before heat, before distractions, the kind of focused effort that rewires you. Afternoons shift: e-bike explorations with whoever's up for it, dinner in villages like Cornudella with the crew, the texture of Mediterranean light, wine that costs seven euros and tastes like attention. Nights you're present, tired in the right way, telling stories that matter because they happened that day.

Why this over, say, a big swell-trip push or a peak bagging expedition: Climbing at that level of intensity, sustained over weeks, forces vulnerability in ways surfing and alpine don't. You can't hide from poor technique or fear on limestone. Simultaneously, you're learning to lead *and* learning to be fully available to your family in a non-English-speaking place where the old routines don't apply — that's the edge. You can't check out into the pursuit because the people you love are right there, also translating, also uncertain, also present. The push isn't purely physical; it's about integrating intensity with attention to the people around you.

Avoid: Making this a boys' trip. Avoid the climbing camps or guidebook-per-crag tourism. Avoid rushing through multiple regions — you need depth, not a highlight reel. Avoid timing it as a secondary piece of a broader European circuit. And avoid the trap of thinking you need a beach component; the villages have pools, the light is coastal-quality, and if you need surf, that's a different trip.

What makes this the year's defining push: You'll come back tighter as a climber, fluent in a new style, and — this is the real proof — your family will have a three-week story they'll reference forever. That's not softening the pursuit; that's integrating it. That's closing the gap you always sense between who you are and who you're becoming.
