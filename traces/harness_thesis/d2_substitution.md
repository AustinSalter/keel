## CRITIQUE PASS (Iteration 3)

### Elevation Search

The compression identified a gradient of frame-selection difficulty — from automatable low-level to durably valuable high-level. But this gradient model obscures a more fundamental issue: **the relationship between legibility and value in AI systems**.

The most defensible harness innovations are precisely those that are hardest to articulate. This connects to Polanyi's paradox — "we know more than we can tell." The thesis has been operating as though frame engineering is an explicit, teachable practice (write better CLAUDE.md files, encode process not conclusions, select the right manifold). But the practitioners who actually extract asymmetric value from AI systems may be doing something closer to *tacit knowledge deployment* — they have intuitions about when to push, when to restart, when a model is confabulating vs. genuinely reasoning, that they couldn't write down as skills even if they tried.

**The elevation**: *Is the thesis conflating two different activities — explicit frame engineering (which is teachable, scalable, and therefore not defensible) and tacit judgment deployment (which is defensible but not scalable)?* If so, the thesis's practical prescription ("build better harnesses") addresses the wrong one.

### What's Missing After Three Iterations

1. **The role of feedback loops in frame calibration**: The thesis treats frame selection as a one-shot decision (choose your context, then reason). But in practice, frame quality is only revealed *after* reasoning produces output. The real skill is iterative frame adjustment — noticing that outputs are mediocre and shifting frames mid-process. This is a cybernetic process, not a search process, and it requires a theory of *online frame adaptation* that the thesis doesn't have.

2. **Scaling laws for harness quality**: The thesis claims harness is the bottleneck but provides no model for how harness quality scales. Does it scale with the designer's expertise? With iteration count? With context budget? Is there a power law or a sigmoid? Without a scaling model, the claim that "harness improvement has more marginal returns than model improvement" is untestable.

3. **The social epistemology dimension**: The thesis is entirely individualistic — one person, one agent, one context window. But most valuable knowledge production is *social*. Peer review, adversarial collaboration, division of cognitive labor. The dialectic loop simulates social epistemology within a single agent, but it may be a pale imitation of actual multi-perspectival reasoning. The question isn't just "is handoff lossy?" but "does genuine intellectual diversity produce something that simulated diversity cannot?"

4. **Ground truth remains unaddressed**: The 25% meta-level elevation finding means 25% of cases reached different conclusions. "Different" is not "better." Without ground truth evaluation (did the elevated conclusion lead to better predictions, better investments, better decisions?), the thesis's central empirical finding is ambiguous.

### Decision

**CONTINUE** — Iteration 3 of minimum 6. The tacit-vs-explicit distinction and the online frame adaptation gap are new tensions that need development. The thesis is becoming more precise but the practical prescriptions are becoming less clear — a sign that we're approaching a genuine crux rather than a superficial one.

**Next priority**: Investigate whether the tacit/explicit distinction maps onto the thesis's bifurcation between frame provision and process provision, or whether it cuts across both. And address the ground truth problem — without it, the entire empirical foundation is weaker than it appears.
